AI Product Management Case Study Campus Atlas

AI Product Strategy · Evaluation Framework · GTM Roadmap · Working Prototype  ·  Higher Education AI Platform

Product Vision
Universities run on 15+ disconnected systems. Students waste hours navigating registrar, library, dining, housing, and admin — each with its own portal, password, and queue. Campus Atlas replaces that friction with a single AI-native command center that understands what a student needs and routes them there instantly.
The Problem
Students interact with 8–12 separate university systems to complete basic tasks — course registration, library holds, dining plans, housing maintenance, financial aid queries. Each system is siloed. Admin teams receive thousands of repetitive support tickets that could be resolved instantly with the right information surfaced at the right moment. The university is not unintelligent — its intelligence is just inaccessible.
PM Insight
The wedge is the question students ask most — "How do I...?" — not the answer. Every campus has knowledge; none have retrieval. A RAG-based copilot trained on official university documents gives students instant, citation-backed answers without burdening admin staff. That is the 0 to 1 product. The platform expands from there.
Users
Students (primary) · University admin teams · Service dept heads · Product/IT stakeholders
Wedge
AI Copilot — answers student questions from official university docs with citations and confidence scores
Expand
Student Command Center → Services Hub → Admin Dashboard → Escalation Workflows
Moat
University-specific RAG corpus + human escalation feedback loop improves accuracy over time per institution

AI Architecture
Ingestion Layer — Official university docs chunked and embedded into a vector store
RAG Retrieval — Semantic search returns top-K relevant passages per query
Response Generation — LLM synthesizes answer with source citations
Confidence Threshold — Low-confidence responses route to human escalation, not hallucination
Groundedness Eval — Every response scored against source passages for factual grounding
Active Learning — Escalation decisions feed back into corpus improvement
PM Thinking — Key Decisions
Chose citation-first UX over fluent summaries — students need to trust answers, not just receive them
Escalation over hallucination — below 65% confidence, the copilot says "I'm not sure, here's who to ask" instead of guessing
Admin dashboard scoped for Phase 2 — wedge with students first; admin adoption follows when ticket volume drops visibly
Institutional GTM — sell to VP of Student Affairs, not IT; they own the budget and the user pain
Metrics Framework
Groundedness Rate >= 90% — answers tied to source documents
Escalation Precision >= 80% — escalations are genuinely ambiguous, not noise
Admin Ticket Deflection — primary business impact metric, visible to VP-level buyers
Student Resolution Time — time to answer drops from avg 2 days to under 30 seconds
Roadmap
MVP — RAG copilot, citation UI, escalation flow, groundedness eval
Phase 2 — Services Hub (dining, library, housing integrations)
Phase 3 — Admin dashboard, ticket analytics, corpus curation tools
Phase 4 — Multi-institution deployment, shared policy corpus

TrustShield AI
AI-Powered Trust & Safety Moderation Platform
Product Vision
Modern content platforms must balance scale, safety, and transparency. TrustShield AI helps moderation teams detect harmful content faster — while keeping humans in control and decisions explainable.
The Problem
Short-form video platforms struggle to detect harmful content quickly while maintaining transparency in moderation decisions. Moderation teams act on flags they can't explain — eroding reviewer trust, slowing appeals, and creating legal risk for platforms.
The Solution
TrustShield AI is an AI-assisted moderation platform that helps trust and safety teams detect harmful content using explainable signals and human-in-the-loop workflows. The core insight: moderation teams don't just need a flag — they need to know why the model flagged it, how confident it is, and when to override. The product is built for trust, not just accuracy.

Case Study · 03

Platform Product Case Study Cross-Continental
Data Pipeline

Analyst-Facing Eco-Economic Intelligence Platform  ·  5 Countries  ·  4 APIs  ·  PostgreSQL  ·  Flask REST API

Product Vision
Policy analysts, risk officers, and researchers spend 60–70% of their time acquiring and cleaning data from fragmented government APIs — leaving little time for the analysis that drives decisions. The product eliminates that wrangling by delivering a unified, query-ready eco-economic warehouse designed around analytical questions, not raw data schemas.
The Problem
A researcher studying drought impact on food prices must pull from Open-Meteo (weather), World Bank (GDP/inflation), EIA (energy), and FAO (commodity prices) — each with different authentication, schemas, and update frequencies. There is no shared platform. Every research question requires rebuilding the same data plumbing from scratch. The bottleneck is not analysis capability — it is data access.
PM Decision
Built for the analyst, not the data engineer. API response schema was designed around analytical questions ("How did weather anomalies in Nigeria affect food prices?") — not around source system schema. No analyst should need to write a JOIN. Modular connector architecture means adding a new data source never touches existing pipelines.
Users
Policy Analysts · NGO Research Leads · Academic Researchers · Supply-Chain Risk Officers
Countries
USA · Brazil · India · Philippines · Nigeria — chosen for cross-source data availability
Sources
Open-Meteo (weather) · World Bank (GDP/inflation) · EIA (energy) · FAO (food prices)
Scope
Deferred: real-time ingestion, UI layer, predictive modeling — until historical data quality validated
Python ETL PostgreSQL Flask REST API Docker Platform Product Data Engineering PM Case Study
GitHub ↗ Discuss This Project
System Architecture
Ingestion Layer — 4 isolated Python connectors (weather.py, world_bank.py, eia_energy.py, food_prices.py); each fails independently
Normalization — Per-source transformation to monthly grain, consistent ISO3 country codes, documented imputation rules
PostgreSQL Warehouse — master.py orchestrates: load CSVs → create master_data table → add constraints; star schema for time-series queries
Flask REST API — Analyst-facing endpoints filterable by country, date range, indicator type; no SQL knowledge required
Orchestration — Cron + Docker Compose; run_incremental.sh handles delta updates without full reloads
PM Thinking — Key Decisions
Modular connectors over monolithic ETL — a new data source shouldn't touch existing pipelines; critical for a research platform where the data universe keeps expanding
Data lineage as a product feature — every imputation decision is traceable; analysts can see how a value was derived, not just what it is
Reproducibility is a trust feature — Docker Compose packages the full stack so an analyst can reproduce a result from 6 months ago even after source corrections
Rejected flat-table approach — query performance at analytical scale matters; an analyst waiting 40 seconds abandons the tool
Metrics Framework
Data Freshness < 30 days — policy relevance degrades with stale data
Schema Coverage > 90% — low coverage means analysts discover gaps mid-project
API Response < 150ms P95 — slow queries kill adoption for research workflows
Imputation Rate < 10% — high imputation signals underlying data reliability issues
Roadmap
MVP (Done) — 4-source ingestion, normalization, PostgreSQL warehouse, Flask API, Docker, cron orchestration
Phase 2 — Airflow orchestration, data quality scoring, 20-country coverage, API auth + rate limiting
Phase 3 — Lightweight analyst query UI, partner integrations (UN OCHA, WFP data feeds)
Phase 4 — Predictive module: weather + energy → food price impact forecasting

Product Thinking

How I think about hard problems —Principles.

Four things I've learned building products at the intersection of AI, systems, and people.

01
AI Changes What Decisions Are Worth Making
AI doesn't just automate tasks — it changes which decisions are worth making at the product level. Building TrustShield AI taught me that the real PM challenge with AI products isn't the model: it's deciding what confidence threshold justifies removing human judgment, and designing the workflow around that boundary. Most AI product failures happen because that question never gets asked.
02
System Visibility Is Itself a Product Decision
At Mercedes-Benz, I watched six engineering pods work toward the same release with different mental models of "ready." No individual was wrong — the system had no shared visibility layer. The most impactful product decision isn't always a feature. Sometimes it's a dashboard that gives distributed teams the same picture of reality at the same time. Alignment is a product outcome.
03
User Insight Beats Feature Ideas Every Time
KaraOrchee's most important product decision came from discovery, not ideation. Users at Peabody didn't want an "AI music app" — they wanted practice that felt coached. That reframe changed the positioning, the feature set, and the pricing model. The best product decisions I've made weren't clever — they were honest responses to what users actually said when given space to be specific.
04
The Frame Is the Product
Photography taught me that a great shot isn't about the subject — it's about what you choose to include and exclude. Product management is the same. The way you define a problem shapes every decision downstream: what gets built, what gets cut, what gets measured. Most product failures aren't execution failures — they're framing failures. Getting the frame right is the most underrated PM skill.
05
Balance Automation with Human Judgment
The most dangerous AI product decision is removing humans from the loop too early. TrustShield AI taught me this directly: moderation accuracy alone doesn't build trust — explainability and override capability do. The right question isn't "can the model decide this?" but "at what confidence level should a human stay involved?" Designing that boundary is a PM responsibility, not an ML one.
